Adoptee Citizenship Action

As international adoptees (or parents of an adopted child), it is encouraged to double-check your citizenship status and ensure you have the necessary documentation to prove that you are U.S. citizens.

Even if you possess a valid passport, it is recommended that you also have your Naturalization Certificate or Certificate of Citizenship. While a U.S. passport can be used to prove citizenship, it is not a USCIC-issued document that officially determines citizenship status.

Additionally, you may have confusion as to which documents you should have, depending on when you were adopted.

This tool will help international adoptees determine their citizenship and the steps to ensure they have the correct papers.

You're all set!

You are a U.S. citizen

Good job having all your ducks in a row. Your Naturalization Certificate or Certificate of Citizenship is proof of your citizenship, so make sure to keep it in a safe place!

If you need a copy of your certificate to submit for things like visa applications or other foreign government uses, you can get a "certified true copy" by going to your local USCIS office. Find out more on the USCIS website.

File an N-565 Request to Get a Replacement

Your Naturalization Certificate is proof of your U.S. citizenship.

Visit U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services to learn how to file a request for a replacement certificate.

According to USCIS, a photocopy of the certificate of naturalization or citizenship is not a valid document for most legal purposes.

If you need a copy of your certificate to submit for things like visa applications or other foreign government uses, you can get a "certified true copy" by going to your local USCIS office. Find out more on the USCIS website.

File an N-600 Application

Your Certificate of Citizenship is proof of your U.S. citizenship.

While you likely acquired citizenship automatically under the Child Citizenship Act of 2000, proof of citizenship in the form of a certificate was not automatically issued.

Visit U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services to download the form and get instructions for applying for a Certificate of Citizenship.

Read the fact sheet for more information on the CCA2000.

You may be an adoptee without citizenship

According to the U.S. Department of State:

"Children who were 18 or older on February 27, 2001, did NOT acquire U.S. citizenship under the Child Citizenship Act. In recent years, many adoptees have found that although they were legally adopted and have been U.S. residents for most of their lives, they do not hold U.S. citizenship."

Contact the Adoptee Rights Campaign and/or your agency for information and support.

It is also recommended that you seek council from an immigration attorney at your convenience.
